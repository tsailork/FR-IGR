Phantom Pressure Regularization (PPR)

A Complete Architectural & Mathematical Implementation Guide for High-Order Flux Reconstruction (FR) Solvers

1. Complete Theoretical Summary

High-order collocated spatial discretization methods, such as Flux Reconstruction (FR) and Discontinuous Galerkin (DG), possess minimal intrinsic numerical dissipation. While this property is highly desirable for capturing turbulent structures and acoustic waves, it renders the solver severely susceptible to Gibbs oscillations and explosive numerical instabilities near shocks, contact surfaces, and under-resolved steep gradients.

The Phantom Pressure Regularization (PPR) method—also categorized as a self-targeted Hyperbolic Bulk Viscosity (HBV) model—introduces a non-physical, passive auxiliary variable \(S = \rho P_{phan}\) that behaves as a localized physical damper. The physical governing system (3D Navier-Stokes or Euler equations) is augmented with an advection-relaxation equation for this phantom pressure state:

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (S \mathbf{u}) = \frac{\rho}{\tau} (P_{phys} - P_{phan})$$

where \(P_{phys}\) is the standard thermodynamic pressure, \(P_{phan} = S/\rho\) is the phantom pressure, \(\mathbf{u}\) is the fluid velocity vector, and \(\tau\) is a localized, non-equilibrium relaxation time scale.

The Physical Mechanism of Hyperbolic Bulk Viscosity

Applying a first-order Chapman-Enskog expansion to the auxiliary transport equation under the assumption of small non-equilibrium deviations (\(\tau \to 0\)) yields:

$$P_{phan} \approx P_{phys} + \tau \gamma P_{phys} (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) + \mathcal{O}(\tau^2)$$

By defining a regularized pressure \(P_{reg}\) as a linear extrapolation of the physical pressure away from the phantom state:

$$P_{reg} = P_{phys} + \theta (P_{phys} - P_{phan})$$

and substituting the Chapman-Enskog expansion, we reveal:

$$P_{reg} \approx P_{phys} - \underbrace{\theta \tau \gamma P_{phys}}_{\beta_{bulk}} (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u})$$

This formulation is mathematically equivalent to the physical pressure modified by an artificial dynamic bulk viscosity \(\beta_{bulk} = \theta \tau \gamma P_{phys}\).

  • In Smooth Regions: The relaxation rate is fast (\(\tau \to 0\)), driving \(P_{phan} \to P_{phys}\), collapsing \(P_{reg} \to P_{phys}\). No artificial dissipation is introduced, preserving high-order accuracy.
  • Across Compressive Discontinuities (Shocks): The velocity divergence is highly negative (\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} \ll 0\)). The finite relaxation rate \(\tau\) causes \(P_{phan}\) to lag behind, forcing \(P_{reg}\) to spike significantly above \(P_{phys}\). This localized pressure spike acts as a physical bulk viscous damper, smoothing out high-frequency Gibbs oscillations over the span of cell interfaces.

2. Flux Evaluation at a Point

To implement PPR, the dimension of your solver's conserved state vector \(\mathbf{U}\) must be expanded from 5 to 6. At every solution point, flux point, and integration point within your 3D domain, the local physical fluxes must be computed using the regularized pressure \(P_{reg}\) instead of the standard thermodynamic pressure \(P_{phys}\).

Step-by-Step State Vector and Flux Construction

Given the augmented conserved state vector:

$$\mathbf{U} = \begin{bmatrix} U_0 \\ U_1 \\ U_2 \\ U_3 \\ U_4 \\ U_5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \rho \\ \rho u \\ \rho v \\ \rho w \\ E \\ S \end{bmatrix}$$

The pointwise regularized pressure is calculated as follows:

  1. Compute the local kinetic energy and thermodynamic physical pressure \(P_{phys}\): $$P_{phys} = (\gamma - 1) \left( U_4 - \frac{1}{2 U_0} \left( U_1^2 + U_2^2 + U_3^2 \right) \right)$$
  2. Extract the pointwise phantom pressure \(P_{phan}\): $$P_{phan} = \frac{U_5}{U_0}$$
  3. Evaluate the regularized pressure \(P_{reg}\) using the scaling parameter \(\theta\): $$P_{reg} = P_{phys} + \theta (P_{phys} - P_{phan})$$

The 3D inviscid physical flux tensor \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{U}) = [\mathbf{F}_x, \mathbf{F}_y, \mathbf{F}_z]\) is constructed using \(P_{reg}\) for the momentum and energy equations, and the advected phantom variable \(S\) in the 6th row:

$$\mathbf{F}_x = \begin{bmatrix} \rho u \\ \rho u^2 + P_{reg} \\ \rho u v \\ \rho u w \\ (E + P_{reg}) u \\ S u \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{F}_y = \begin{bmatrix} \rho v \\ \rho u v \\ \rho v^2 + P_{reg} \\ \rho v w \\ (E + P_{reg}) v \\ S v \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{F}_z = \begin{bmatrix} \rho w \\ \rho u w \\ \rho v w \\ \rho w^2 + P_{reg} \\ (E + P_{reg}) w \\ S w \end{bmatrix}$$

3. General Riemann Solver Considerations

High-order FR schemes compute common interface normal fluxes \(\mathbf{F}^{comm} \cdot \mathbf{n}\) at cell boundaries using extrapolated interior (\(\mathbf{U}^-\)) and exterior (\(\mathbf{U}^+\)) states. Since the regularized pressure \(P_{reg}\) varies dynamically near shocks, the interface Riemann solver must be carefully modified to prevent numerical shock-induced instabilities.

Acoustic Wave Speed Corrections

At interfaces near strong shocks, the physical pressure can drop or fluctuate due to transient under-resolved waves, while \(P_{reg}\) exhibits a stabilizing positive spike. To prevent non-physical wave speeds or complex speed-of-sound evaluations, we define a safe upper-bound pressure state at both sides of the interface:

$$\bar{P}^- = \max\left(P_{reg}^-, P_{phys}^-\right), \quad \bar{P}^+ = \max\left(P_{reg}^+, P_{phys}^+\right)$$

These corrected pressures are then utilized to compute the local speeds of sound:

$$a^- = \sqrt{\gamma \frac{\bar{P}^-}{\rho^-}}, \quad a^+ = \sqrt{\gamma \frac{\bar{P}^+}{\rho^+}}$$

The maximum normal wave speed \(\lambda_{max}\) at the interface is computed as:

$$\lambda_{max} = \max\left( |\mathbf{u}^- \cdot \mathbf{n}| + a^-, \, |\mathbf{u}^+ \cdot \mathbf{n}| + a^+ \right)$$

Rusanov (Local Lax-Friedrichs) Interface Flux

The common interface normal flux is evaluated using the augmented flux functions containing the regularized pressure:

$$\mathbf{F}^{comm} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{U}^-) + \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{U}^+) \right) \cdot \mathbf{n} - \frac{1}{2} \lambda_{max} \left( \mathbf{U}^+ - \mathbf{U}^- \right)$$

The sixth variable \(S\) is naturally stabilized and upwinded at the interface by the scalar dissipation term:

$$- \frac{1}{2} \lambda_{max} \left( S^+ - S^- \right)$$

4. Operator Splitting and Point-Wise Analytical Relaxation Method

Because the relaxation time scale \(\tau \to 0\) in smooth regions, integrating the stiff source term \(\frac{\rho}{\tau}(P_{phys} - P_{phan})\) using standard explicit Runge-Kutta stages will severely violate the CFL condition and cause instant numerical blowups.

To maintain both high-order accuracy and unconditional numerical stability, we implement a **fractional-step operator splitting** method. The governing equations are split into a homogeneous hyperbolic operator and a local ODE relaxation operator:

Pointwise vs. Cell-Average Calculation of the Relaxation Time \(\tau\)

The local relaxation time scale \(\tau\) determines how quickly the phantom pressure \(P_{phan}\) relaxes to the physical thermodynamic pressure \(P_{phys}\). It must be proportional to the local advective/acoustic time scale of the grid cell. It is computed as:

$$\tau = \frac{C_{\tau} \cdot \Delta x}{\lambda_{loc} + \epsilon}$$

where the terms are defined as follows:

  • \(C_{\tau}\): A user-defined tuning coefficient (typically in the range of \(0.1 \le C_{\tau} \le 0.5\)). Smaller values make the relaxation faster (sharper shocks but less stable), while larger values broaden the shock profile.
  • \(\Delta x\): The local characteristic grid spacing. For 3D unstructured meshes, this is commonly computed as the ratio of the cell volume to its maximum face area: \(\Delta x = V_e / \max(A_{face})\), or simply the minimum edge length of the element.
  • \(\lambda_{loc}\): The local maximum wave speed, defined as the sum of the velocity magnitude and speed of sound: $$\lambda_{loc} = \|\mathbf{u}\| + a = \sqrt{u^2 + v^2 + w^2} + \sqrt{\gamma \frac{P_{phys}}{\rho}}$$
  • \(\epsilon\): A small regularizing parameter (e.g., \(10^{-12}\)) to prevent division by zero in stagnant regions.
High-Order Implementation Best Practice: While it is mathematically possible to compute \(\tau\) pointwise at every single high-order solution point, it is highly recommended to compute a single cell-averaged constant value \(\tau_e\) per element. Allowing \(\tau\) to vary wildly within a single high-order element introduces artificial, non-physical gradients that degrade the spatial accuracy of the Flux Reconstruction scheme.

The Fractional-Step Integration Sequence

For each Runge-Kutta sub-stage with step size \(\Delta t_{stage}\):

  1. Explicit Spatial Step: Solve the homogeneous spatial advection step using your standard high-order FR operators: $$\frac{\partial \mathbf{U}}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{U}) = 0 \quad \implies \quad \mathbf{U}^* = \mathbf{U}^n - \Delta t_{stage} \mathcal{D}_h(\mathbf{U}^n)$$
  2. Analytical Relaxation Step: Integrate the pointwise stiff source ODE using the intermediate state \(\mathbf{U}^*\) as the starting condition: $$\frac{d\mathbf{U}}{dt} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ \frac{\rho}{\tau}(P_{phys} - P_{phan}) \end{bmatrix}$$

Mathematical Derivation of the Point-Wise Update

Because the source terms for mass (\(\rho\)), momentum (\(\rho \mathbf{u}\)), and total energy (\(E\)) are exactly zero, these physical quantities remain completely constant during this local ODE relaxation step. Consequently, the local physical pressure \(P_{phys}\) remains strictly constant.

The ODE for the phantom state \(S = \rho P_{phan}\) simplifies to a linear, decoupled, constant-coefficient equation:

$$\frac{d S}{d t} = \frac{1}{\tau} (\rho P_{phys} - S)$$

Integrating this ODE analytically from \(t\) to \(t + \Delta t_{stage}\) yields the exact, unconditionally stable update formula:

$$S^{n+1} = \rho P_{phys} + \left(S^* - \rho P_{phys}\right) e^{-\Delta t_{stage} / \tau}$$

5. Boundary Conditions

Imposing strong boundary conditions (e.g., directly overwriting interface values) in high-order FR solvers causes spurious acoustic reflections that trigger solver crashes. To guarantee stability, all boundary conditions must be enforced weakly using specifically constructed Ghost States (\(\mathbf{U}^+\)) in combination with the interface Riemann solver.

Implementation Rule: The phantom pressure state \(S^+\) at any physical boundary must be actively synced to the local boundary pressure. This prevents non-physical bulk-viscosity forces and numerical boundary layers from developing at the domain boundaries.

A. Subsonic and Supersonic Outflow Boundaries

Let \(\mathbf{U}^-\) be the interior state extrapolated to the outflow boundary interface, and \(\mathbf{n}\) be the outward-pointing unit normal.

  • Supersonic Outflow (\(\mathbf{u}^- \cdot \mathbf{n} \ge a^-\)): All characteristics travel out of the domain. No boundary specifications are mathematically allowed. Construct the ghost state as a direct copy of the interior: $$\mathbf{U}^+ = \mathbf{U}^-$$
  • Subsonic Outflow (\(0 < \mathbf{u}^- \cdot \mathbf{n} < a^-\)): Exactly one acoustic characteristic travels upstream into the domain. We must prescribe a target physical backpressure \(P_{out}\), while density, momentum, and phantom pressure are smoothly extrapolated from the interior. $$\mathbf{U}^+ = \begin{bmatrix} \rho^- \\ (\rho u)^- \\ (\rho v)^- \\ (\rho w)^- \\ \frac{P_{out}}{\gamma - 1} + \frac{1}{2}\rho^- \|\mathbf{u}^-\|^2 \\ \rho^- P_{out} \end{bmatrix}$$ Setting \(S^+ = \rho^- P_{out}\) guarantees that \(P_{reg} \to P_{out}\) smoothly at the exit plane without generating artificial pressure spikes.

B. Inflow / Dirichlet Boundaries

For a physical state with targeted inflow properties \([\rho_{\infty}, \mathbf{u}_{\infty}, P_{\infty}]^T\), construct the ghost state as:

$$\mathbf{U}^+ = \begin{bmatrix} \rho_{\infty} \\ \rho_{\infty} u_{\infty} \\ \rho_{\infty} v_{\infty} \\ \rho_{\infty} w_{\infty} \\ \frac{P_{\infty}}{\gamma - 1} + \frac{1}{2}\rho_{\infty} \|\mathbf{u}_{\infty}\|^2 \\ \rho_{\infty} P_{\infty} \end{bmatrix}$$

C. Adiabatic Solid Walls (Slip & No-Slip)

To enforce zero-penetration at physical wall boundaries, construct a reflective ghost state. The normal velocity is negated, while density, tangential velocity, and phantom state are extrapolated symmetrically:

$$\mathbf{u}^+ = \mathbf{u}^- - 2(\mathbf{u}^- \cdot \mathbf{n})\mathbf{n}$$
$$\mathbf{U}^+ = \begin{bmatrix} \rho^- \\ \rho^- \mathbf{u}^+ \\ E^- \\ S^- \end{bmatrix}$$

6. Interactive Code & Verification Block

The following complete Python snippet implements the pointwise flux evaluation, interface Riemann solver wave speed correction, weak boundary conditions, and the point-wise analytical relaxation loop.

import numpy as np def compute_ppr_pointwise_flux(U, theta, gamma): """ Evaluates the physical 3D flux vector at a specific solution point. U: numpy array [rho, rhou, rhov, rhow, E, S] """ rho = U[0] rhou, rhov, rhow = U[1], U[2], U[3] E = U[4] S = U[5] u = rhou / rho v = rhov / rho w = rhow / rho ke = 0.5 * (rhou*u + rhov*v + rhow*w) # 1. Compute physical and phantom pressures P_phys = (gamma - 1.0) * (E - ke) P_phan = S / rho # 2. Regularized pressure formulation P_reg = P_phys + theta * (P_phys - P_phan) # 3. Construct 3D flux components Fx = np.array([rhou, rhou*u + P_reg, rhou*v, rhou*w, (E + P_reg)*u, S*u]) Fy = np.array([rhov, rhov*u, rhov*v + P_reg, rhov*w, (E + P_reg)*v, S*v]) Fz = np.array([rhow, rhow*u, rhow*v, rhow*w + P_reg, (E + P_reg)*w, S*w]) return Fx, Fy, Fz, P_phys, P_reg def compute_rusanov_flux(U_L, U_R, normal, theta, gamma): """ Evaluates interface Rusanov flux incorporating safe maximum wave speed limits. """ Fx_L, Fy_L, Fz_L, Pp_L, Pr_L = compute_ppr_pointwise_flux(U_L, theta, gamma) Fx_R, Fy_R, Fz_R, Pp_R, Pr_R = compute_ppr_pointwise_flux(U_R, theta, gamma) Fn_L = Fx_L*normal[0] + Fy_L*normal[1] + Fz_L*normal[2] Fn_R = Fx_R*normal[0] + Fy_R*normal[1] + Fz_R*normal[2] # Safe wave speed limit using upper bound pressure P_safe_L = max(Pp_L, Pr_L) P_safe_R = max(Pp_R, Pr_R) a_L = np.sqrt(gamma * P_safe_L / U_L[0]) a_R = np.sqrt(gamma * P_safe_R / U_R[0]) un_L = (U_L[1]*normal[0] + U_L[2]*normal[1] + U_L[3]*normal[2]) / U_L[0] un_R = (U_R[1]*normal[0] + U_R[2]*normal[1] + U_R[3]*normal[2]) / U_R[0] lambda_max = max(abs(un_L) + a_L, abs(un_R) + a_R) # Common interface normal flux evaluation Fn_comm = 0.5 * (Fn_L + Fn_R) - 0.5 * lambda_max * (U_R - U_L) return Fn_comm def apply_pointwise_relaxation(U_star, dt_stage, tau, gamma): """ Executes the exact, unconditionally stable pointwise analytical relaxation update. This must be executed on all solution points after EVERY Runge-Kutta sub-stage. """ rho = U_star[0] ke = 0.5 * (U_star[1]**2 + U_star[2]**2 + U_star[3]**2) / rho P_phys = (gamma - 1.0) * (U_star[4] - ke) S_eq = rho * P_phys exp_factor = np.exp(-dt_stage / tau) # Update passive scalar dynamically U_star[5] = S_eq + (U_star[5] - S_eq) * exp_factor return U_star
